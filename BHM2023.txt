    <h4>Camp Creek Warriors Present:</h4>
    <img src="https://pbs.twimg.com/profile_images/1258422620678209537/MLV-AlOJ_400x400.jpg" class="smaller">
    <div id="logo"><img class="logo" src="https://cache.bsnsports.com/comp/sls/get/1/1189?f=jpg&sid=1189&ts=1638206562"></div>
    <h1>Game changers in education</h1>
    <div class="dropdown">
      <button class="dropbtn">Rita Pierson</button>
      <div class="dropdown-content">
        <p>Gave one of the most powerful Ted Talks on education – “Every Child Needs a Champion” has become a motto for countless educators. Additionally, she led workshops on the importance of educating young African American boys.</p><embed width="260" height="215" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/SFnMTHhKdkw" frameborder="0" allow="accelerometer; autoplay; clipboard-write; encrypted-media; gyroscope; picture-in-picture" allowfullscreen="">
        </div></div>
  <div class="dropdown">
    <button class="dropbtn">Carlotta Walls LaNier</button>
    <div class="dropdown-content">
      <p>As the youngest member of the Little Rock Nine, Carlotta Walls LaNier braved harassment-and even the bombing of her home- to be one of the first black students to attend Central High School in 1960. She wrote a book about the experience: A Mighty Long Way: My Journey to Justice at Little Rock Central High School.  She was also president of the Little Rock Nine Foundation which aimed to continue to create equal educational opportunities for all students.</p><img src="https://www.prhspeakers.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/10/LaNier_-Carlotta_-Walls_940_529_72-ppi.jpg">
  </div></div>
<div class="dropdown">
  <button class="dropbtn">Charlotte Forten Grimke</button>
  <div class="dropdown-content">
    <p>The first black teacher to work at the Penn School in South Carolina, a school established to help educate newly freed black slaves after the Civil War. Later, she worked with the US Treasury Department to help recruit black educators. </p><img src="https://www.nps.gov/common/uploads/place/nri/20161213/places/BF4B0F9F-1DD8-B71B-0B7A8309A09898C9/BF4B0F9F-1DD8-B71B-0B7A8309A09898C9.jpg">
</div></div>
<div class="dropdown">
  <button class="dropbtn">Fanny Jackson Coppin</button>
  <div class="dropdown-content">
    <p>Was the first African American principal. Born as a slave, she once wrote to Frederick Douglas,</p><img src="https://static.wixstatic.com/media/c5c288_e93935f94e6e45ba8642ee3d1a516d04~mv2.jpg/v1/fill/w_224,h_336,al_c,lg_1,q_80,enc_auto/c5c288_e93935f94e6e45ba8642ee3d1a516d04~mv2.jpg">
</div></div>
<div class="dropdown">
  <button class="dropbtn">Inez Beverly Prosser</button>
  <div class="dropdown-content">
    <p>The first black woman to earn a Ph.D. in Psychology, Inez Beverly Prosser did extensive research on the effect of segregated schools versus non-segregated schools on African American students. She was passionate about finding the best way to foster and educate young black students. She was also one of the first to take a vested interest in the mental health of African American children subjected to racism.</p>
    <embed src="https://player.vimeo.com/video/386505475?h=3ebe8db369&amp;color=ffffff&amp;title=0&amp;byline=0&amp;portrait=0" width="640" height="360" frameborder="0" allow="autoplay; fullscreen; picture-in-picture" allowfullscreen="">
</div></div>
<div class="dropdown">
  <button class="dropbtn">Dr. Jeanne L. Noble</button>
  <div class="dropdown-content">
    <p>A professor of education, Dr. Jeanne L. Noble was active in trying to desegregate her hometown of Augusta, GA.  She was appointed by three presidents (Johnson, Nixon and Ford) to serve on educational commissions.</p>
    <img src="https://static.wixstatic.com/media/c5c288_2db2ee6038d347a4a7e9b117d007ba08~mv2.jpg/v1/fill/w_374,h_263,al_c,lg_1,q_80,enc_auto/c5c288_2db2ee6038d347a4a7e9b117d007ba08~mv2.jpg">
</div></div>
<div class="dropdown">
    <button class="dropbtn">Mary McLeod Bethune</button>
    <div class="dropdown-content">
      <p>Became one of the most important civil rights leaders of the twentieth century. She was a lifelong educator- working as a teacher before founding Bethune-Cookman college which set educational standards for today’s black colleges. She went on to become the highest-ranking African American woman in government when President Franklin Roosevelt named her director of Negro Affairs of the National Youth Administration.</p><img src="https://evolve-success.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/08/MMB-hero-sm-image-1024x689.jpg">
    </div></div>
  <div class="dropdown">
      <button class="dropbtn">Septima Poinsette Clark</button>
      <div class="dropdown-content">
        <p>When she first began her teaching career, Septima Poinsette Clark was frustrated that Charleston did not hire black educators. She worked with the NCAAP to petition the city to change its policies. She went on to help run citizenship schools which taught African Americans basic literacy and math so they could pass the test required to register to vote. </p><img src="https://kinginstitute.stanford.edu/sites/mlk/files/septima_fitch.jpg">
      </div></div>
    <div class="dropdown">
        <button class="dropbtn">Marva Collins</button>
        <div class="dropdown-content">
          <p>Started Westside Preparatory School in 1975 where she earned a reputation for teaching “unteachable” students. She trained thousands of educators on her techniques which focused heavily on the Socratic method. In 2004, she received the National Humanities Medal. </p>
          <img src="https://static01.nyt.com/images/2015/06/29/us/29COLLINS-obit/29COLLINS-obit-superJumbo.jpg">
        </div></div>
      <div class="dropdown">
          <button class="dropbtn">Edmund Gordon</button>
          <div class="dropdown-content">
            <p>As a founder of the federal Head Start program, Dr. Edmund Gordon was one of the first educators to focus on closing the academic achievement gap. He also founded the Institute for Urban Education at Columbia University’s Teachers College.</p><embed width="560" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/pStryMmtjFM" frameborder="0" allow="accelerometer; autoplay; clipboard-write; encrypted-media; gyroscope; picture-in-picture" allowfullscreen="">
          </div></div>
        <div class="dropdown">
            <button class="dropbtn">Charles Hamilton Houston</button>
            <div class="dropdown-content">
              <p>Was a dean at Howard University Law School and argued cases in the U.S. Supreme Court, creating a legal foundation for the historic Brown v. Board of Education decision. He was involved in just about every civil rights case that was argued in front of the supreme court from 1930 and 1954, earning him the nickname “The Man Who Killed Jim Crowe.”</p><img src="https://www.tc.columbia.edu/media/news/images/2021/april/ewg_portrait_hero_img.jpg">
            </div></div>
